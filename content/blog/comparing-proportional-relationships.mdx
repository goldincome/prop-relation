You get two job offers. Job A pays \$60 for 4 hours. Job B pays \$72 for 6 hours. Which one pays better per hour?

Both are proportional relationships — each follows $y = kx$ with no added fees or base pay. But they have different $k$ values. Your job is to find which $k$ is larger and decide.

Here's how to compare proportional relationships in any format.

## The Rule: Bigger $k$ Wins

Every proportional relationship has a constant of proportionality $k$. In a comparison, **the relationship with the larger $k$ is always faster, steeper, pricier, or denser** depending on the context.

$$y = k_1x \quad \text{vs} \quad y = k_2x$$

If $k_1 > k_2$, then for the same $x$, $y_1 > y_2$. That's the entire idea.

## Comparing from Two Tables

Find $k$ for each table by dividing $y$ by $x$. Then compare.

| $x$ | Job A: $y$ |
| :-: | :-: |
| 4 | \$60 |
| 6 | \$90 |
| 8 | \$120 |

| $x$ | Job B: $y$ |
| :-: | :-: |
| 4 | \$48 |
| 6 | \$72 |
| 8 | \$96 |

Job A: $k = 60 \div 4 = 15$. Job B: $k = 48 \div 4 = 12$.

$$k_A = 15, \quad k_B = 12$$

$15 > 12$, so Job A pays more per hour (\$15/hr vs \$12/hr).

**Check the units.** $k$ is in dollars per hour. That tells you what you're actually comparing.

### Another Table Example

Compare these two speed relationships:

| Time (hr) | Car X: Distance (mi) |
| :-: | :-: |
| 2 | 120 |
| 5 | 300 |

| Time (hr) | Car Y: Distance (mi) |
| :-: | :-: |
| 3 | 135 |
| 7 | 315 |

Car X: $k = 120 \div 2 = 60$ mph. Car Y: $k = 135 \div 3 = 45$ mph.

Car X is faster. Its $k$ is larger.

## Comparing from Two Graphs

**The steeper line has the larger $k$.**

A graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line through $(0, 0)$. The slope of that line *is* $k$. Steeper slope = larger $k$ = faster change.

Compare two lines:

- Line A passes through $(2, 6)$ → $k = 6 \div 2 = 3$
- Line B passes through $(2, 4)$ → $k = 4 \div 2 = 2$

Line A is steeper. Its $k$ is larger.

If both lines are on the same set of axes, the one that rises faster (is more vertical) wins. No calculation needed — just look.

**One warning:** the axes must use the same scale. A graph with a compressed $x$-axis can make a slow relationship look steep.

## Comparing from Two Equations

This is the easiest case. $k$ is the coefficient of $x$.

| Equation | $k$ |
| --- | :-: |
| $y = 4x$ | 4 |
| $y = 2.5x$ | 2.5 |
| $y = \frac{5}{2}x$ | 2.5 |

Which equation represents the greater constant of proportionality?

$$y = 8x \quad \text{vs} \quad y = \frac{1}{2}x$$

$8 > \frac{1}{2}$. First equation wins.

What about fractions and decimals?

$$y = \frac{7}{3}x \quad \text{vs} \quad y = 2.3x$$

Convert or compute: $\frac{7}{3} \approx 2.33$. $2.33 > 2.3$. The first wins — barely.

## Comparing Across Formats: Table vs Graph vs Equation

You won't always get two tables or two graphs. Sometimes you get one of each. The method is always the same: **find $k$ and compare.**

| Source | Data | $k$ |
| --- | --- | :-: |
| Table | $x = 4, y = 20$ | $20 \div 4 = 5$ |
| Graph | Line through $(3, 12)$ | $12 \div 3 = 4$ |
| Equation | $y = 4.5x$ | 4.5 |

Ranking from largest $k$ to smallest: table ($k = 5$) > equation ($k = 4.5$) > graph ($k = 4$).

The process never changes:

1. Extract $k$ from each representation.
2. Put them side by side.
3. Pick the largest.

## Real-World Comparisons

**Job offers.** Two friends compare summer jobs. Miguel earns $y = 14x$. Priya's earnings table shows \$44 for 4 hours ($k = 11$). Miguel earns more per hour ($14 > 11$).

**Grocery prices.** Store A: \$3 for 12 ounces ($k = 0.25$ per ounce). Store B: $y = 0.30x$ ($k = 0.30$ per ounce). Store A is cheaper.

**Typing speeds.** A graph shows Kara's line passing through $(5, 200)$ — she types 40 words per minute. Lin's equation is $y = 55x$ — 55 words per minute. Lin is faster.

## Key Insight

The constant of proportionality $k$ converts "how many units of $x$" into "how many units of $y$." A larger $k$ means more $y$ per $x$. That translates to:

- **Higher pay** per hour
- **Faster speed** per hour
- **Higher cost** per ounce
- **More production** per batch

Always ask: "What does $k$ measure here?" Then larger = more of that thing.

## FAQ

**How do you compare proportional relationships from a table?**
Find $k$ for each table by dividing $y$ by $x$ using any row where $x \neq 0$. The table with the larger $k$ has the stronger relationship.

**What does it mean if one graph is steeper than another?**
The steeper line has a larger $k$ — a higher constant of proportionality. For the same $x$, the steeper line produces more $y$.

**Can you compare a proportional and a nonproportional relationship?**
Yes, but only for specific $x$ values. A proportional relationship follows $y = kx$; a nonproportional one follows $y = mx + b$. At $x = 0$, the proportional gives $y = 0$ while the nonproportional gives $y = b$. For large $x$, the relationship with the larger coefficient eventually wins.

**What if $k$ is negative in one relationship?**
A negative $k$ means $y$ decreases as $x$ increases. A positive $k$ (even a tiny one like $0.01$) is "larger" than any negative $k$.

**How do you compare proportional relationships in a word problem?**
Extract the $k$ from each situation. A job paying \$18/hour ($k = 18$) pays more than one paying \$15/hour ($k = 15$). A car going 70 mph ($k = 70$) is faster than one going 55 mph ($k = 55$).

**Do the axes need to be the same scale when comparing graphs?**
Yes. Different axis scales can make a slower relationship appear steeper. Always check the units and scale before comparing steepness visually.

**If two proportional relationships have the same $k$, are they the same?**
They have the same rate, but they could represent different contexts. $k = 5$ could mean 5 dollars per hour or 5 miles per hour. The math is identical, but the real-world meaning differs.

**What's the quickest way to compare two proportional relationships?**
Convert each to $y = kx$ form. The equation gives you $k$ directly. For tables and graphs, compute $k$ from one data point. Then compare the numbers.
