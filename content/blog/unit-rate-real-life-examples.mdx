A 24-ounce jar of pasta sauce costs \$3.60. A 15-ounce jar costs \$2.25. Which do you buy?

The unit rate is the single calculation that answers this — and dozens of other real-world questions — in seconds.

A **unit rate** tells you how much per one unit: per ounce, per hour, per gallon, per minute. You find it by dividing the first quantity by the second.

$$\text{Unit rate} = \frac{\text{first quantity}}{\text{second quantity}}$$

Here are 15 real-life unit rate examples your students will actually remember.

## Shopping: Price Per Unit

### 1. Price Per Ounce

A 24-ounce jar of pasta sauce costs \$3.60.

$$\frac{\$3.60}{24 \text{ oz}} = \$0.15 \text{ per oz}$$

A 15-ounce jar costs \$2.25. $\$2.25 \div 15 = \$0.15$ per ounce. Same unit rate — buy whichever size fits your meal. Most grocery stores list this on the shelf tag for a reason.

### 2. Price Per Item

A 12-pack of soda costs \$6. A 6-pack costs \$3.50.

- 12-pack: $\$6 \div 12 = \$0.50$ per can
- 6-pack: $\$3.50 \div 6 \approx \$0.58$ per can

The 12-pack saves you 8 cents per can. Bulk isn't always cheaper — but here it is.

### 3. Price Per Pound

Apples are \$3.60 for 3 pounds.

$$\frac{\$3.60}{3 \text{ lb}} = \$1.20 \text{ per lb}$$

You pay \$1.20 per pound. If you buy 5 pounds, expect to pay $5 \times \$1.20 = \$6.00$.

## Speed: Distance Per Time

### 4. Miles Per Hour

You drive 150 miles in 3 hours on the highway.

$$\frac{150 \text{ miles}}{3 \text{ hours}} = 50 \text{ mph}$$

At that unit rate, you'd cover 200 miles in 4 hours. Speed limits are unit rates — they tell you the maximum distance per hour.

### 5. Feet Per Second

A ball rolls 48 feet in 3 seconds.

$$\frac{48 \text{ ft}}{3 \text{ sec}} = 16 \text{ ft/sec}$$

That's the speed of a moderate throw. Compare it to a ball that rolls 60 feet in 5 seconds (12 ft/sec) — and you know which is faster.

## Cooking: Quantities Per Serving

### 6. Cups Per Batch

A pancake recipe uses 4 cups of mix for 8 servings.

$$\frac{4 \text{ cups}}{8 \text{ servings}} = 0.5 \text{ cups per serving}$$

Each serving needs half a cup. Want 12 servings? $12 \times 0.5 = 6$ cups of mix.

### 7. Tablespoons Per Serving

A salad dressing recipe needs 6 tablespoons of vinegar for 12 servings.

$$\frac{6 \text{ tbsp}}{12 \text{ servings}} = 0.5 \text{ tbsp per serving}$$

That's half a tablespoon — or $1\frac{1}{2}$ teaspoons — per serving.

## Wages: Money Per Time

### 8. Dollars Per Hour

You earn \$72 for working 6 hours.

$$\frac{\$72}{6 \text{ hours}} = \$12 \text{ per hour}$$

At \$12 per hour, an 8-hour shift pays $8 \times \$12 = \$96$. A friend earning \$15 per hour makes \$120 for the same shift. The unit rate tells you who earns more, regardless of hours worked.

## Fuel Efficiency: Distance Per Volume

### 9. Miles Per Gallon

A car travels 270 miles on 9 gallons of gas.

$$\frac{270 \text{ miles}}{9 \text{ gallons}} = 30 \text{ mpg}$$

A truck that goes 180 miles on 9 gallons gets only 20 mpg. The unit rate lets you compare any two vehicles side by side.

## Reading Speed: Pages Per Time

### 10. Pages Per Minute

A student reads 200 pages in 50 minutes.

$$\frac{200 \text{ pages}}{50 \text{ minutes}} = 4 \text{ pages per minute}$$

At that rate, they'll finish 60 pages in 15 minutes. Use the unit rate to estimate how long any book will take.

## Manufacturing: Units Per Time

### 11. Units Per Hour

A factory produces 240 toy cars in 8 hours.

$$\frac{240 \text{ toy cars}}{8 \text{ hours}} = 30 \text{ toy cars per hour}$$

Over a 40-hour workweek, that's $40 \times 30 = 1,200$ toy cars. Factory managers live and die by unit rates like this.

## Construction: Cost Per Area

### 12. Cost Per Square Foot

A 400-square-foot room costs \$800 to carpet.

$$\frac{\$800}{400 \text{ sq ft}} = \$2 \text{ per sq ft}$$

A 600-square-foot room at the same rate costs $600 \times \$2 = \$1,200$. The unit rate scales any project instantly.

## Travel: Cost Per Mile

### 13. Cost Per Mile

An 8-mile taxi ride costs \$24.

$$\frac{\$24}{8 \text{ miles}} = \$3 \text{ per mile}$$

A 15-mile ride would cost $15 \times \$3 = \$45$. Compare this to a \$2.50-per-mile ride to find the cheaper option.

## Printing: Pages Per Time

### 14. Pages Per Minute

A printer outputs 180 pages in 6 minutes.

$$\frac{180 \text{ pages}}{6 \text{ minutes}} = 30 \text{ pages per minute}$$

A 60-page document prints in 2 minutes. A second printer at 20 pages per minute takes 3 minutes for the same job. The unit rate decides which machine is faster.

## Data Plans: Cost Per GB

### 15. Cost Per GB

A phone plan gives you 10 GB of data for \$60.

$$\frac{\$60}{10 \text{ GB}} = \$6 \text{ per GB}$$

A plan with 20 GB for \$100 costs $5 per GB. Lower unit rate, better deal — as long as you actually use the data.

## Why These Examples Stick

Each example above follows the same pattern:

1. Identify the two quantities (cost and ounces, miles and hours)
2. Divide the first by the second
3. Write the answer as "per one unit"

Once students see the pattern across shopping, speed, cooking, and wages, they stop memorizing formulas and start applying the logic.

## FAQ

**What is a unit rate in simple terms?**
A unit rate tells you how much per one unit. Miles per hour, dollars per pound, and pages per minute are all unit rates.

**How do you find a unit rate?**
Divide the first quantity by the second. The answer is your unit rate. $$\frac{\$15}{3 \text{ lb}} = \$5 \text{ per lb}$$

**Whats the difference between a rate and a unit rate?**
A rate can have any number as the second quantity (60 miles per 2 hours). A unit rate always has 1 (30 miles per 1 hour).

**Can a unit rate be less than 1?**
Yes. 50 cents per ounce and 0.5 cups per serving are both unit rates under 1.

**When would I use a unit rate in real life?**
Every time you compare prices at the grocery store, calculate your car's gas mileage, figure out your hourly wage, or estimate how long a trip will take.

**Do unit rates always use division?**
Yes. You always divide the first quantity by the second to get a unit rate.

**Whats the most common mistake when calculating unit rates?**
Dividing the wrong way. Price per ounce means cost ÷ ounces, not ounces ÷ cost.

**Why do grocery stores show unit prices on shelf tags?**
So you can compare different package sizes without doing the math yourself. The shelf tag already shows the unit rate.
