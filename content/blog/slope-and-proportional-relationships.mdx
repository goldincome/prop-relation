A wheelchair ramp rises 1 foot for every 12 feet of horizontal run. The steepness of that ramp — 1/12 — is its slope. If the ramp starts at ground level (height 0 at distance 0), that same number is also the constant of proportionality.

Slope and the constant of proportionality are the same number when a line passes through the origin. When it doesn't, they split apart. Here's exactly how that works.

## What Slope Means

Slope measures steepness. It's the ratio of vertical change to horizontal change:

$$\text{slope} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Take two points on a line, subtract their coordinates, and divide. The result tells you how much $y$ changes for each 1-unit increase in $x$.

| Points | Rise | Run | Slope |
|---|---|---|---|
| $(2, 6)$ and $(5, 15)$ | $15 - 6 = 9$ | $5 - 2 = 3$ | $9 \div 3 = 3$ |
| $(1, 4)$ and $(3, 10)$ | $10 - 4 = 6$ | $3 - 1 = 2$ | $6 \div 2 = 3$ |
| $(0, 0)$ and $(4, 12)$ | $12 - 0 = 12$ | $4 - 0 = 4$ | $12 \div 4 = 3$ |

Same slope every time. That's what makes a line straight.

## When Slope Equals $k$

A proportional relationship is a line through the origin: $y = kx$. The slope formula on this line gives:

$$\text{slope} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{kx_2 - kx_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{k(x_2 - x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} = k$$

The $k$ factors out and cancels the denominator. Slope is $k$.

**On a proportional line, slope, unit rate, and the constant of proportionality are the same number.** Three names, one value.

## When Slope Does Not Equal $k$

The moment a line shifts off the origin — $y = kx + b$ with $b \neq 0$ — the relationship stops being proportional, and slope and unit rate become different numbers.

Take $y = 3x + 2$:
- **Slope:** $3$ (the line still rises 3 units per 1 unit right)
- **Unit rate:** Varies. At $x = 1$: $y = 5$, rate is $5$. At $x = 2$: $y = 8$, rate is $4$. Not constant.

The line has the same steepness. But the $+2$ means $y$ is always 2 more than $kx$, so $y \div x$ changes depending on which $x$ you pick. No single unit rate. No proportionality.

## Rise Over Run as Unit Rate

On a proportional graph, pick any point $(x, y)$ where $x \neq 0$. The rise from the origin to that point is $y$, and the run is $x$.

$$\text{slope} = \frac{y}{x} = k$$

This is why every point on a proportional line gives the same $y/x$ ratio. Each step right adds exactly $k$ units up.

## Real-World Slope as Steepness

Slope isn't just a math formula. It describes real things:

- **Ramps:** A 1:12 ramp has slope $1/12 \approx 0.083$. Gentle.
- **Stairs:** Building codes require rise/run between $0.5$ and $0.7$. Steeper than most ramps.
- **Roofs:** A "6/12 roof" rises 6 inches per 12 inches of run. Slope $= 0.5$.
- **Highway grades:** A 7% grade means 7 feet of vertical per 100 feet horizontal. Slope $= 0.07$.

When the starting point is zero (ground level, flat ground, bottom of the hill), these slopes are also unit rates.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1:** A line passes through $(0, 0)$ and $(7, 28)$. Find the slope and the constant of proportionality.

$$m = \frac{28 - 0}{7 - 0} = \frac{28}{7} = 4$$

$k = 4$. The equation is $y = 4x$.

**Problem 2:** A line passes through $(0, 0)$ and $(9, 6)$. Find the unit rate.

$$\text{unit rate} = \frac{6}{9} = \frac{2}{3}$$

Slope $= 2/3$, unit rate $= 2/3$, $k = 2/3$. All the same.

**Problem 3:** Line A is $y = 2x$. Line B is $y = 2x + 5$. Which has a constant unit rate? Which has a higher $y/x$ ratio at $x = 3$?

Line A is proportional — every point gives $y/x = 2$. Line B gives $11/3 \approx 3.67$ at $x = 3$ and $15/5 = 3$ at $x = 5$. The ratio changes. Line B is not proportional.

## FAQ

**Is slope always the constant of proportionality?**
No. Only when the line passes through the origin. If $y = kx + b$ with $b \neq 0$, slope is $k$ but the constant of proportionality does not exist.

**What's the difference between slope and unit rate?**
Slope is the ratio of rise to run between any two points on a line. Unit rate is $y/x$ for a single point. They're equal on every point of a proportional line and different on a non-proportional line.

**Can slope be negative in a proportional relationship?**
Yes. $y = -3x$ has slope $-3$ and $k = -3$. The line goes through the origin pointing downward.

**How do you find $k$ from slope?**
If the line goes through the origin, $k$ equals the slope. Calculate rise over run from any two points, and that's your $k$.

**What about $y = 0.5x$ — what's the slope?**
$0.5$. Slope is the coefficient of $x$ when the equation is in $y = kx$ form. No calculation needed.

**Can you have slope without a proportional relationship?**
Yes. Every straight line has a slope. Only lines through the origin are proportional.

**Why do 7th-grade standards connect slope and proportionality?**
Because proportional relationships are the simplest kind of linear function. Understanding that slope $= k$ now makes graphing $y = mx + b$ in 8th grade much easier.
