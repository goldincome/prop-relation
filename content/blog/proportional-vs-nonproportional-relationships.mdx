You're comparing two cell phone plans. One charges \$10 per gigabyte of data. The other charges \$5 per gigabyte plus a \$20 monthly base fee.

Both look like straight lines on a graph. Both charge per gigabyte. But one is proportional and the other is not. The difference? That \$20 base fee.

Here's how to tell them apart — every time, in every format.

## The Core Difference in One Sentence

A **proportional** relationship follows $y = kx$ (no added constant). A **nonproportional** relationship follows $y = mx + b$ (shifted by $b$).

That $b$ — the constant added or subtracted — is the only thing separating proportional from nonproportional.

## Side-by-Side: Tables

Compare these two tables. Both are linear. Only one is proportional.

| $x$ | Proportional $y$ | $y \div x$ |
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
| 1 | 5 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 | 5 |
| 3 | 15 | 5 |
| 4 | 20 | 5 |

| $x$ | Nonproportional $y$ | $y \div x$ |
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
| 1 | 7 | 7 |
| 2 | 12 | 6 |
| 3 | 17 | 5.67 |
| 4 | 22 | 5.5 |

Left table: every $y \div x$ equals 5. Constant ratio. $k = 5$. **Proportional.**

Right table: the ratios change (7, 6, 5.67, 5.5). The $y$ values increase by 5 each time, but they start at 7 instead of 5. This is $y = 5x + 2$. **Not proportional.**

**The test:** divide $y$ by $x$. Same every time = proportional. Different = not proportional.

## Side-by-Side: Graphs

Both lines below are straight. Both have the same steepness. Look where they cross the $y$-axis.

- **Proportional:** crosses at $(0, 0)$. Line: $y = 5x$.
- **Nonproportional:** crosses at $(0, 2)$. Line: $y = 5x + 2$.

$$y = 5x \quad \text{vs} \quad y = 5x + 2$$

Same slope. Same angle. But that 2-unit shift up changes everything. A line through the origin is proportional. A line shifted off the origin — even by 0.1 — is not.

**The test:** check $(0, 0)$. If the line goes through it, check ratio consistency. If not, done — not proportional.

## Side-by-Side: Equations

| Proportional | Nonproportional |
|---|---|
| $y = 3x$ | $y = 3x + 4$ |
| $y = \frac{1}{2}x$ | $y = \frac{1}{2}x - 3$ |
| $y = -2x$ | $y = -2x + 1$ |
| $y = x$ | $y = x + 5$ |

Left column: $y = kx$ form only. No $+ b$, no $- b$. Right column: has a constant term that shifts the line.

**The test:** can you write it as $y = kx$ with nothing added or subtracted? If yes, proportional. If you need $+$ or $-$, it's not.

## Why the Origin Matters

The origin rule comes straight from the equation. In $y = kx$:

$$y = k(0) = 0$$

Plug in $x = 0$, and $y$ must be 0. In $y = mx + b$:

$$y = m(0) + b = b$$

Plug in $x = 0$, and $y = b$. That $b$ is the shift.

In real terms, $b$ means "something is already there before you start." That's a base fee, a starting charge, an initial distance — anything that exists at zero quantity. Proportional relationships have none of that. Zero of one means zero of the other.

## Real-World Examples

**Proportional:** Gas costs \$4 per gallon. Buy 0 gallons = pay \$0. Buy 5 gallons = pay \$20. Buy 10 = \$40. Every gallon adds exactly \$4.

$$y = 4x$$

**Nonproportional:** A cell plan costs \$5 per GB plus a \$20 base fee. Use 0 GB = still pay \$20. Use 5 GB = pay \$45 (\$25 data + \$20 fee). Use 10 GB = pay \$70. The fee doesn't multiply.

$$y = 5x + 20$$

The fee turns a proportional relationship into a nonproportional one. That \$20 is $b$.

## Quick Reference

| Feature | Proportional | Nonproportional |
|---|---|---|
| Equation form | $y = kx$ | $y = mx + b$ ($b \neq 0$) |
| Graph | Straight line through $(0, 0)$ | Straight line shifted off origin |
| Table ratios | Constant ($y \div x$ same) | Changing ($y \div x$ differs) |
| $x = 0$ gives | $y = 0$ | $y = b$ |
| Real example | Pay per hour with no base | Pay per hour with sign-up fee |

## Practice: Identify Proportional or Not

**1. Table:**

| $x$ | $y$ |
| :-: | :-: |
| 2 | 8 |
| 5 | 20 |
| 7 | 28 |

$8 \div 2 = 4$, $20 \div 5 = 4$, $28 \div 7 = 4$. Constant ratio. **Proportional.** $k = 4$.

**2. Graph:** A straight line passes through $(0, 3)$ and $(2, 7)$.

The line doesn't pass through $(0, 0)$. **Not proportional.** (It's $y = 2x + 3$.)

**3. Equation:** $y = \frac{3}{4}x - 2$

Has a $- 2$ constant. **Not proportional.**

**4. Word problem:** A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 12 cookies. Write the relationship.

$$k = 2 \div 12 = \frac{1}{6}$$

$$y = \frac{1}{6}x$$

No added constant. When the baker makes 0 cookies, they use 0 cups of flour. **Proportional.**

## FAQ

**Is a nonproportional relationship still linear?**
Yes. $y = mx + b$ is linear — it graphs as a straight line. The "nonproportional" label means it doesn't pass through the origin, not that it's curved.

**Can a relationship be proportional if $k$ is negative?**
Yes. $y = -3x$ is proportional. The line goes through the origin and has a constant ratio ($-3$). It just points downward.

**What if a table has $x = 0$ and $y = 0$ but ratios differ later?**
Not proportional. The origin is necessary but not sufficient. You still need every $y \div x$ to match. Check non-zero rows to be sure.

**How do I know $b$ from a graph?**
Look at the $y$-intercept — where the line crosses the $y$-axis. That point is $(0, b)$. If $b \neq 0$, it's nonproportional.

**What if the equation is $y = 5x + 0$?**
That's just $y = 5x$. Proportional. $b = 0$ means no shift. Most teachers won't write the $+ 0$, but if they do, it's still proportional.

**Do all proportional relationships have a $y$-intercept of 0?**
Yes. In $y = kx$, when $x = 0$, $y = 0$. The line always passes through $(0, 0)$. That's the definition.

**Can a nonproportional relationship have a constant $y \div x$?**
No. If $y \div x$ is constant, the relationship is $y = kx$ by definition, which means $b = 0$ and it's proportional.

**What's the difference between $k$ and $m$?**
Nothing, when the line goes through the origin. In $y = kx$, $k$ is the slope. In $y = mx + b$, $m$ is the slope. Same number, same meaning — the rate of change. The only difference is the $b$.
