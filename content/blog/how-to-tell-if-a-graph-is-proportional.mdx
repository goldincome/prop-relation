You're looking at a graph on a test. Two lines. One goes through $(0, 0)$, the other starts at $(0, 2)$. They look almost the same. Which one is proportional?

## The Two Rules for Proportional Graphs

A graph shows a proportional relationship when it meets **both** conditions:

1. **The graph is a straight line.**
2. **The line passes through the origin $(0, 0)$.**

That's it. If either condition fails, the relationship is not proportional.

### Rule 1: Straight Line

A proportional relationship has a constant rate of change. On a graph, that always looks like a straight line — no curves, no bends.

✅ Proportional: A straight line.
❌ Not proportional: A curve, parabola, or any non-linear shape.

### Rule 2: Through the Origin

This is the rule students miss most. A straight line that doesn't pass through $(0, 0)$ is **not** proportional — even though it looks almost identical.

The origin rule comes from the equation $y = kx$. When $x = 0$, $y$ must be $0$:

$$y = k(0) = 0$$

If there's any constant added ($y = kx + b$), then when $x = 0$, $y = b \neq 0$. The line shifts up or down, and proportionality breaks.

## Visual Examples

**Proportional graph:** A straight line through $(0, 0)$ ascending at a constant angle — like $y = 2x$.

**Non-proportional graph (trap #1):** A straight line through $(0, 2)$ ascending at the same angle — like $y = 2x + 2$. Same slope, shifted up. Not proportional.

**Non-proportional graph (trap #2):** A curved line that happens to pass through the origin — like $y = x^2$. Passes through $(0, 0)$, but it's not a straight line. Not proportional.

## The Point Test

If you only have a graph (no table, no equation), you can still check proportionality by picking points.

1. Pick any two points on the line where $x \neq 0$.
2. For each point, divide $y$ by $x$.
3. If both divisions give the same number, the graph is proportional.

For a graph of $y = 3x$:
- Point $(1, 3)$: $3 \div 1 = 3$
- Point $(2, 6)$: $6 \div 2 = 3$

Same ratio. Proportional. For $y = 3x + 1$:
- Point $(1, 4)$: $4 \div 1 = 4$
- Point $(2, 7)$: $7 \div 2 = 3.5$

Different ratios. Not proportional.

## Why This Matters in Math Class

Proportional graphs are the foundation for slope, linear functions, and eventually calculus. Every 7th-grade standards-aligned curriculum tests this exact skill:

- Is the line straight?
- Does it go through the origin?
- What is the constant of proportionality (the slope)?

## FAQ

**Can a proportional graph be a horizontal line?**
Only if it's $y = 0$ (the x-axis). A horizontal line like $y = 4$ has slope 0 and doesn't pass through the origin.

**Can a proportional graph be a vertical line?**
No. A vertical line like $x = 3$ isn't a function, and it doesn't pass through $(0, 0)$.

**What if the graph shows points that aren't connected?**
The points should form a straight line pattern through the origin. If they do, the data represents a proportional relationship.

**Does the line have to go through $(0, 0)$ exactly?**
Yes. Even a line that goes through $(0, 0.1)$ fails the origin test. In math class, precision matters.

**How is slope related to proportional graphs?**
In a proportional graph, the slope equals the constant of proportionality $k$. Read more in [slope and proportional relationships](/blog/slope-and-proportional-relationships).
